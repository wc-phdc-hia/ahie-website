Healthcare Implementations

The reference implementations which form part of the AHIE model are tested methodologies which are transferable and can be adopted and adapted in other regions as context-appropriate solutions.

Healthcare applications identified for inclusion in these reference implementations were developed or matured within the project, with the objective of utilising interoperability technology to access the consolidated data and the consolidated data environments.

With a focus on HIV and TB patients as the primary beneficiaries of the initiative, applications which were currently widely deployed and government-run, and which supported the HIV and TB healthcare management of these patients, were given priority. These applications include TIER.Net, NHLS, OpenIHP/SPV amongst others.

The following use cases were designed to establish the feasibility of patient-level data exchange utilising the technology stack proposed:

HPRS – NHLS interoperability

The specific use case selected to test this was integration with the NHLS to alert clerks to the availability of laboratory results on patient arrival and to handle the hand-off to the appropriate laboratory results portal. Once this functionality was matured in HPRS, it could provide the basis for further lookups for clinical data from other repositories. In addition, alerting functionality could be added for additional events, such as for patients who were lost-to-care elsewhere on the platform, especially for conditions with transmission risk, such as TB and drug-resistant TB.

HPRS - TIER.Net interoperability

TIER.Net is an HIV and TB patient information system used from facility level through to national level in more than 3,500 facilities in South Africa. The system had already been adapted to interoperate with standards-compliant patient master indices such as the HPRS. Establishing consistent interoperability between HPRS and TIER.Net would ensure that the unique Health Patient Registration Number (HPRN) generated and held by HPRS was used as an identifier on TIER.Net. This interoperability would demonstrate at scale that users of the health system who were registered on HPRS could be linked to nationally standardized patient information systems such as TIER.Net where appropriate. Given the already wide existing footprint of TIER.Net, this would facilitate the rapid scaling up of interoperability components.

TIER.Net real-time interoperability with the PHDC via the OpenLHIM

TIER.Net would be adapted to provide appropriate messaging to the PHDC via the OpenLHIM and similarly consume and integrate responses. The anticipated result would be automatic integration of laboratory results into TIER.Net, the identification of encounters elsewhere for patients who were lost to follow-up and the submission of data about clinical encounters more frequently than the current quarterly dispatching. As part of the AHIE project, TIER.Net would be further extended to share clinical data bi-directionally, based on messaging standards. The system would still be able to function in an offline environment but would also be able to benefit from connectivity where this was available, even if intermittently. This enhancement would provide a valuable parallel approach to the development of fully online systems.

TIER.Net – NHLS interoperability

To improve the fidelity of facility-derived operational and patient management reports, laboratory results were critically needed in local instances of TIER.Net. The reports would identify patients who required interventions, such as adherence interventions where viral loads were persistently elevated, or retention or re-engagement in care for patients who had missed appointments or disengaged from TB or HIV care. It was anticipated that TIER.Net – NHLS interoperability, whether it was direct (in the case of facilities that were online) or via the HIE would help mitigate one of the significant weaknesses of facility-based HIV and TB data, which was the failure to file, record and capture laboratory results. Interoperability for this reference implementation would be via the HIE.

PHDC - Automated data consolidation, routinely available reports and a prototype single patient viewer (SPV)

The proposed technology stack would allow off-line capability while still providing the advantages of data exchange, e.g. demographic and laboratory data, when and where possible.
