Unique Patient Identifier

A crucial components of the AHIE project is the use of a unique patient identifier, which enables patient-level data from multiple sources in the Health Information Exchange environment to be linked.

The scale up of a unique national patient identifier, through the implementation of the Health Patient Registration System (HPRS), presented a unique opportunity for South Africa to leapfrog from a context of largely absent or disconnected patient registration systems, to one in which patients share a unique health identifier across all public sector health services.

All clinical data which carry this identifier, irrespective of when they were digitised, can be reliably linked on this identifier to other data in the health exchange or data centre environment, as is the case in the Western Cape through the Patient Master Index (PMI).

Note: Where patient records do not include a unique patient identifier the linking of patient data from disparate data sources is still possible through the data harmonisation and curation strategy of the PHDC, via the use of quasi-identifiers such as name, surname, address and ID number.

Background

At the start of the AHIE project the HPRS was already in active national deployment, having been implemented in over 1000 facilities, with 3 million patients registered. The HPRS was supported by a team of engineers at CSIR who had architected and developed the solution with a view to robust scalability in the South African context.

It was estimated that by the end of the project 15 million clients of the health system would be registered on the HPRS and linked via unique identifiers to their records in nationally standardised patient information systems such as TIER.net and NHLS.

    

The following use cases were designed to establish the feasibility of patient-level data exchange utilising the HPRS / PMI:

HPRS – NHLS interoperability

The specific use case selected to test this was integration of HPRS with the NHLS to alert clerks to the availability of laboratory results on patient arrival and to handle the hand-off to the appropriate laboratory results portal. Once this functionality was matured in HPRS, it could provide the basis for further lookups for clinical data from other repositories. In addition, alerting functionality could be added for additional events, such as for patients who were lost-to-care elsewhere on the platform, especially for conditions with transmission risk, such as TB and drug-resistant TB.

HPRS - TIER.Net interoperability

TIER.Net is an HIV and TB patient information system used from facility level through to national level in more than 3,500 facilities in South Africa. The system had already been adapted to interoperate with standards-compliant patient master indices such as the HPRS. Establishing consistent interoperability between HPRS and TIER.Net would ensure that the unique Health Patient Registration Number (HPRN) generated and held by HPRS was used as an identifier on TIER.Net. This interoperability would demonstrate at scale that users of the health system who were registered on HPRS could be linked to nationally standardized patient information systems such as TIER.Net where appropriate. Given the already wide existing footprint of TIER.Net, this would facilitate the rapid scaling up of interoperability components.
