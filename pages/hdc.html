Provincial Health Data Centre (PHDC)

A primary requirement of the AHIE project was to establish a set of processes for ingesting, processing and beneficiating data that would be meaningful for managing HIV and TB. As a result there was a strong focus on building national and sub-national population-based health data centres, in which all available person-level data would be harmonised, based on unique patient identification.

Together with the Health patient registration system (HPRS) implementation, which had been rolled out nationally, the data harmonisation and patient master index (PMI) strategy implemented by the Provincial Health Data Centre (PHDC), in the selected incubation region of the Western Cape Province, provided the AHIE project with an opportunity to harness the value of unique patient identifiers.

The PHDC is essentially a repository model, with database repositories for facilities, patients, clinical data providers and community members. Data for clinical domains are abstractions from data sets residing in several source systems such as medicine dispensing software databases or formatted laboratory reports.

Rather than implement parallel systems to record and aggregate data, the adopted model saw aggregate and management reporting occur as a natural by-product of clinical and administrative systems.

For some domains, such as referrals between ﬁxed health services and services provided by community health workers, the PHDC fulﬁls the functions of a Health Information Exchange, through bidirectional standards-based interoperability.

The activities of the PHDC are broadly characterised as harmonisation and curation, data beneﬁciation, and data provision.

Harmonisation

In the harmonisation process, source data is harmonised into the consolidated PHDC architecture.

Data are received through several mechanisms:

  • uploaded via linked database server access
  • pushed to source-speciﬁc holding databases on the PHDC infrastructure
  • loaded through a health information mediator to a holding database
  • fetched from remote drives or ftp server locations
  • pushed to PHDC source-speciﬁc ftp sites
  • manually extracted and uploaded on a schedule

Curation and Data Linkage

In curation of the data, each data point is mapped to a person through linkage on the patient master index or PMI, as well as to a location and a standard ontology, such as ICD10 codes for diagnoses, ATC codes for medicines, and where possible LOINC codes for diagnostic tests.

Data linkage comprises linkage within the PMI to identify all possible PMI duplicates, as well as the linkage of all attribute data from source systems to the PMI. Matches based on canonical identiﬁers (e.g. civil identiﬁer or hospital folder number), with some corroboration, confer higher certainty than those based on other identiﬁers.

Beneficiation

Beneﬁciation involves using all available data to infer health service contacts or encounters, health conditions or episodes and health outcomes for conditions. Rather than relying on high quality coding to determine health conditions, the PHDC strategy infers health conditions by leveraging a range of data sources. This approach categorises outputs based on certainty, with only inferences with high certainty used for clinical tools such as the Single Patient Viewer (SPV).

Data Provision and Governance

The PHDC differs from traditional data linkage centres in that the same environment can service clinical needs based on linked named patient data as well as analytic and research needs based on linked anonymised data. This is due to the PHDC being managed within the provincial government health services. Regarding data provision, data can be released from the PHDC for in-depth epidemiological research and analysis. These releases are strictly governed to ensure adherence to ethical and patient protection prescripts.

PHDC Architecture

The following use cases were designed to establish the feasibility of patient-level data exchange utilising the technology stack proposed:

TIER.Net real-time interoperability with the PHDC via the OpenLHIM

TIER.Net would be adapted to provide appropriate messaging to the PHDC via the OpenLHIM and similarly consume and integrate responses. The anticipated result would be automatic integration of laboratory results into TIER.Net, the identification of encounters elsewhere for patients who were lost to follow-up and the submission of data about clinical encounters more frequently than the current quarterly dispatching. As part of the AHIE project, TIER.Net would be further extended to share clinical data bi-directionally, based on messaging standards. The system would still be able to function in an offline environment but would also be able to benefit from connectivity where this was available, even if intermittently. This enhancement would provide a valuable parallel approach to the development of fully online systems.

PHDC - Automated data consolidation, routinely available reports and a prototype single patient viewer (SPV)

The proposed technology stack would allow off-line capability while still providing the advantages of data exchange, e.g. demographic and laboratory data, when and where possible.
